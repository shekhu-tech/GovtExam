5. Training & Probation üèõÔ∏è

Selected officers undergo a probation period, including intensive induction training at premier institutions.

1. Probation Period

The initial appointment is on a probationary basis.

  • Grade A officers are on probation for 2 years.
  • Grade B officers are on probation for 2 years, which includes 1 year of training.
  • Confirmation is subject to satisfactory performance.

2. Induction Training

Officers are trained at reputed banking institutes.

  • Induction training is conducted at institutions like NBSC, BIRD, etc.
  • Covers rural economics, finance, and NABARD's operational aspects.
  • Includes exposure visits to various rural development projects.

6. Key Responsibilities of an Officer üåü

NABARD officers are involved in a wide spectrum of activities related to the financing and development of India's rural sector.

Grade 'A' (AM - RDBS)

  • Assisting in policy formulation for rural credit.
  • Appraising and sanctioning projects of RRBs/Co-op Banks.
  • Monitoring the implementation of rural development schemes.

Grade 'A' (Specialists)

  • Handling legal matters and documentation (Legal).
  • Managing translation and implementation of Hindi (Rajbhasha).
  • Overseeing security arrangements (Protocol & Security).

Grade 'B' (Manager)

  • Leading teams and managing the functions of a department.
  • Interacting with senior officials of banks and state governments.
  • Playing a more significant role in policy and decision-making.

8. Your Preparation Strategy: Smart Work üìà

Acing the NABARD exam requires a specialized strategy, with a strong focus on the ESI and ARD sections, which are part of both Prelims and Mains.

üìö Key Steps:

  1. Master ESI & ARD

    These two subjects are the key. Cover them exhaustively from standard sources, government reports (Economic Survey, Union Budget), and NABARD's annual reports.

  2. Develop Descriptive Writing Skills

    Regularly practice writing essays, letters, and answers for the descriptive papers. Focus on structure, content, and clarity.

  3. Don't Ignore Qualifying Sections

    While the prelims have qualifying sections, you must clear their cut-offs. Maintain regular practice for these subjects.

üîÑ Habits & Practice:

  • Read Business Newspapers: Follow publications like The Hindu, Livemint, or Economic Times to build a strong base for ESI.
  • Follow PIB & Kurukshetra: The Press Information Bureau (PIB) and magazines like Kurukshetra are vital for government scheme-related questions.
  • Attempt Mock Tests: Take mock tests that replicate the unique pattern of the NABARD exam, including both objective and descriptive components.
  • Prepare for the Interview: Develop a strong bio-data and be prepared for questions on agriculture, rural economy, and your personal background.

Phase I: Preliminary Exam

This is a 2-hour composite test of 200 marks. It has a qualifying section (Reasoning, English, Quant, Computer, Decision Making) and a merit section (GA, ESI, ARD). The merit section score is used for shortlisting to Phase II.

Phase II: Main Exam

This phase has two papers. Paper-I is a common General English descriptive test. Paper-II is stream-specific; for RDBS, it's an objective and descriptive test on Economic & Social Issues (ESI) and Agriculture & Rural Development (ARD).

Phase III: Interview

Candidates shortlisted from Phase II are called for a personality test. The interview assesses the candidate's knowledge, personality, and suitability for a career in rural development. The final merit list is based on the combined score of Phase II and the Interview.
